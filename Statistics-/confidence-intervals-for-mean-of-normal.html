Confidence Intervals for Mean of Normal

We have already used confidence intervals for the mean of a normal distribution. Now, we will merely generalize the idea.

Two-Sided Confidence Interval



You have some population with unknown mean, $\mu$, and known standard deviation, $\sigma$. You take a sample of $n$ people and the sample has a mean of $\bar{x}$. You want to construct a $(1-\alpha) \times 100\%$ confidence interval with equal confidence of falling either below or above that interval: From the last example, we know that $\frac{\bar{x}-\mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}$ is a pivotal statistic for a normal distribution and that it follows a standard-normal distribution: $$N(\mu, \frac{\sigma^2}{n})$$ Thus, we can compute $q_1$ and $q_2$ by using the standard normal's inverse and obtain: $$1-\alpha = P\left(q_1 \leq \frac{\bar{x}-\mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} \leq q_2\right)$$ $$1-\alpha = P\left(\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}q_1 \leq \bar{x}-\mu \leq \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}q_2\right)$$ $$1-\alpha = P\left(-\bar{x}+\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}q_1 \leq -\mu \leq -\bar{x}+\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}q_2\right)$$ $$1-\alpha = P\left(\bar{x}-\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}q_1 \geq \mu \geq \bar{x}-\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}q_2\right)$$ Because, the normal distribution is symmetric and the area on both sides of the confidence interval are equal, we know that $q_1=-q_2$, so this simplifies to $$1-\alpha = P\left(\bar{x}+\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}q_2 \geq \mu \geq \bar{x}-\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}q_2\right)$$ Which yields an interval of bounded by $$\bar{x} \pm \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$$
