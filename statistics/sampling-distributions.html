General Idea

I have a fair coin. I flip it 3 times. How many times will heads be flipped? The expected number of heads is 1.5. After all, half the time a coin is flipped, it lands heads, and half of 3 is 1.5. However, in reality, we can only have an integer number of heads, so we will probably see 1 or 2 heads. However, there is also the chance that all 3 flips will be heads and that none of the flips will be heads.

To figure out how likely all of these scenarios are we can use a pretty simple method: just consider all the possibilities:

This is called a sampling distribution. It represents how likely a statistic is to take on certain values given some sample-size (in this case, 3) and some model (in this case, that the coin if fair).

Example: Maximum of the Uniform

Let's say we have a continuous uniform distribution from 0 to $b$ What is the sampling distribution for the maximum of the sample for some sample-size, $n$? $$P\left(m \leq max\left(\{X_1,...,X_n\}\right)\right)$$ by independence, this is equal to $$P\left(m \leq X_1\right) \cdots P\left(m \leq X_n\right)$$ by plugging in the cdf for the uniform distribution, we get $$\frac{m}{b} \cdots \frac{m}{b}, m \leq b$$ which just equals $$\frac{m^n}{b^n}, m \leq b$$ Thus, the cdf for the sampling distribution of the maximum is: $$P\left(m \leq max\left(\{X_1,...,X_n\}\right)\right) = \frac{m^n}{b^n}, m \leq b$$ Taking the derivative gives us the pdf: $$f(m)=\frac{nm^{n-1}}{b^n}, m \leq b$$

Normal Distributions: Mean

Let's say we take an infinite number of $n$-size samples from a normal distribution with some $\mu$ and some $\sigma$. It turns out that the distribution of the sample means will also be normal with $\mu'=\mu$ and $\sigma'=\frac{\sigma}{n}$.

Notice, that the standard deviation of the sampling distribution get's smaller as the sample-size ($n$) gets bigger.

Normal Distributions: Standard Deviation
